Gerunds

A gerund phrase, just like a gerund, acts as a noun in a sentence. Gerunds are formed by adding “-ing” to the verb: “dancing,” “singing,” “speaking.” A gerund is a noun that starts with a verb that acts as a noun. Here are the examples:

Getting married is exciting.

Exercising is healthy.

Washing clothes is tedious.

Getting a good grade was the result of hard work.

There are six types of gerunds:

1 Subject: Biking is my newest hobby.

2 Subject complement: My preferred commuting method is biking.

3 Direct object: I love biking.

4 Indirect object: My sister gave biking a try.

5 Object complement: I see myself biking every day.

6 Object of a preposition: I blocked out a few hours this Sunday for biking.

You should use a gerund when you’re discussing something that’s specific, real, or has already happened:

The attic was absolutely perfect for writing.

An infinitive

When you need to use a verb as a noun, you can also turn it into an infinitive. An infinitive is the word “to” + a verb’s root.

An infinitive is the verb form that has “to” at the beginning. For example, “to do,” “to sleep,” “to love” and “to create.”

There are two ways you can use an infinitive: as a noun that communicates a single concept and as an adjective to describe a noun within the sentence. Here are a few examples, with infinitives:

To err is human. (noun)

To wait seemed foolish. (noun)

I brought us some lemonade to drink with lunch. (adjective)

Everyone wanted to go (Object)

He bought some flowers to give to his wife. (Action)

Do you understand what to do? (Question)

We are ready to play now. (Adverb) (What, who, where) Giving meaning or modify the particular

Your duty is to finish the work in time. (Subject compliment)

An easy rule to keep in mind is that you should use an infinitive when you’re discussing something that’s abstract, not real, or only might happen:

That’s where she plans to propose.
